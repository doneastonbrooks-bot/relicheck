Behind the MBA program case study: how a 240-student program found 26 percentage points of response rate

ReliCheck team April 28, 2026 7 min read

One of our newer customer stories follows an MBA program that switched to ReliCheck for end-of-term course evaluations. The headline is that response rates climbed from 41% to 67% across one term. The detail underneath is more interesting: the response-rate jump came from four specific changes, three of which had nothing to do with the platform itself.

The full story is on the customer story page. This post unpacks the design choices that made the difference, for any team running a course evaluation or a similar end-of-term instrument.

What changed in the design

1. The instrument got shorter

The previous instrument had 24 items. The redesigned version has 18, with the cuts coming from three places: redundant items the inter-item correlation matrix flagged, items that measured instructor personality rather than instruction quality, and one demographic item that the program office did not actually use in any analysis.

Cutting six items dropped the average completion time from 7 minutes to under 4. Response rate elasticity to length is steep in this band: from 7 minutes down, every minute saved tends to add three to five percentage points of response rate.

2. The questions were grouped by dimension, not by topic

Items measuring the same underlying dimension were placed together (clarity items together, alignment items together, climate items together, assessment items together). The previous instrument interleaved them, which forced respondents to switch context every question. Grouping is one of the cheapest design changes available; it costs nothing and reliably reduces respondent fatigue.

3. The reminder schedule changed

Previous schedule: one announcement at survey open, one reminder at the midpoint, one at 24 hours before close. New schedule: announcement at open, a peer-driven nudge from the program office at the midpoint with the current response rate, a personal email from the program director at 48 hours before close to anyone who had not responded, and a final 4-hour reminder.

The personal email from the program director is the change that mattered most. Response rates among the prior-day non-responders jumped 19 percentage points in the 48 hours after that email. None of the previous reminders had attribution; the new one did.

4. The closing message changed

The thank-you screen used to read "Thank you for completing the survey." The new version reads "Your response is in. The program office reviews every response and your faculty member will see the closed-item summaries and a sample of open-ended themes within 7 days." The new version makes the work the response will do explicit. We expect this affected next-term response rates more than this-term.

What changed in the analysis

5. Reliability statistics joined the report header

The previous report led with course means. The new report leads with the four dimension scores plus the alpha tile per dimension. Faculty saw, on the first page, that the items measuring (for example) "clarity of instruction" were holding together, which made the dimension score believable. Pushback from faculty about whether the instrument actually measured what its labels claimed dropped to roughly zero.

6. Open-ended responses got AI theme extraction

Previously, the program coordinator read every open-ended response and wrote a one-paragraph summary per course. With AI theme extraction, the coordinator reviews the AI's themes (typically 4 per course) and the example quotes attached to each, then approves or revises. The work shrank from a full afternoon to about 20 minutes per term, with no meaningful loss of fidelity.

What did not matter

Two changes the program made did not move the needle. The visual redesign of the survey form had no measurable effect on response rate (respondents complete the survey if they decide to, not if it looks nicer). And offering a small completion incentive in a pilot did not increase response rate enough to justify the budget; the program dropped the incentive in the second cycle and saw no decline.

The takeaway

Switching platforms unlocked the analytical changes (reliability, AI themes, methodology appendix). The response-rate change came from design choices any team could implement on any platform: shorter instrument, grouped items, attributed reminders, explicit closing message. If you are evaluating a tool change to fix a response-rate problem, run the cheap design changes first; they will tell you whether the problem is the platform or the program.
