Running a 360 in 15 minutes

ReliCheck team May 14, 2026 9 min read

360 feedback has a reputation for being the survey project that consumes a quarter. Subject lists, rater nominations, separate forms per rater type, reminders that nobody owns, and a final report that an outside consultant assembles for a fee. A useful 360 does not need to take a quarter. The active configuration work is about fifteen minutes, and the report at the end is the same plain-language read whether the project ran for two weeks or twelve.

The walkthrough below is for the HR partner who has been told on a Monday that the new manager cohort wants 360 feedback by the end of the month. Open the 360 Feedback Suite, pick a starter, build the panel, launch invitations, and wait. The fifteen minutes is the active work. The wait for raters to respond is its own thing, and there is a section near the end on what to do during that window.

Before you start: who and what

Two decisions are worth making before the first click. First, the subjects. A 360 is run on people, not on the survey itself. Each subject is one person being evaluated, and the report at the end is one document per subject. Most cohorts run between four and twelve subjects in a single panel. Larger groups still work, the report just lands per subject regardless.

Second, the rater types. The standard 360 distinguishes manager, peer, direct report, external (clients, partners, cross-functional collaborators), and optionally self. Pick the rater types that map to the subject's work. A team lead with no direct reports does not need a direct-report bucket. A senior executive with no formal manager probably needs an external rater bucket instead. ReliCheck supports all five rater types in the same panel and will leave a bucket empty if no raters fall into it.

Minute zero to two: open the suite and pick a starter

Sign in, click Suites in the left workspace nav, and open the 360 Feedback Suite. Six starter templates appear: Manager 360 lite, Leadership feedback, Peer feedback, Team effectiveness, Supervisor evaluation, and Self vs others development review. Each one is a Likert-anchored instrument with a structured open-ended close. The starter to pick depends on who is being evaluated.

  • Manager 360 lite is the default. Twelve items across communication, fairness, coaching, vision, and inclusion, plus two open-ended items at the end. Use it for first-line and middle managers.
  • Leadership feedback is for senior leaders. Extended items on vision, strategy, accountability, and executive presence on top of the core manager items.
  • Peer feedback is the lateral version. Use it when the cohort is being reviewed by their peers in a working group rather than by a chain of command.
  • Supervisor evaluation is for direct-report-only input. It pairs with a Manager 360 panel when you want a clean direct-report subset alongside the multi-rater roll-up.

Click Start from this template on the one that fits. ReliCheck creates the survey, attaches it to the 360 Feedback Suite, and opens the Questions builder. Total elapsed: two minutes.

Minute two to five: customize the items

The starter is opinionated on purpose. Twelve items is a defensible 360 length: long enough to surface a structure across communication, fairness, coaching, and vision, short enough that raters finish in seven to nine minutes. The instinct to add ten more items should be resisted. Each added item costs response rate, and the analytic value of items eleven through twenty is usually modest once items one through twelve have done their job.

Three useful edits at this step. First, replace the placeholder organization name in any item that references it. Second, add or remove items that reflect competencies the cohort is being developed against. If the leadership development program has been training around inclusive decision-making for the last quarter, an inclusive-decision-making item belongs on this 360 even if the starter does not include it. Third, decide whether to include the self-assessment. The Self vs others gap diagnostic in the subject report is one of the most useful artifacts the 360 produces, but only if the subject also takes the survey on themselves.

If a new item feels unclear after writing it, click the AI question review icon to its right. The Construct Mapper will tell you whether the new item belongs with an existing competency cluster or whether it is sitting alone as a one-item construct, which usually points to a wording problem.

Minute five to eight: build the panel

Click the survey's Distribute tab and open the 360 Panels section. A panel binds a single survey to a list of subjects and the raters that go with each subject. Add the subjects first. Name, email, and the relationship anchors live on the subject row. Eight subjects takes about three minutes if the names are in a spreadsheet you can paste from.

Under each subject, list the raters. Most subjects need a manager, three to five peers, and three to five direct reports. The minimum that produces a usable report is two raters per relationship bucket, which lets the per-relationship aggregation work without identifying any single rater. If a subject has only one peer they trust to give honest feedback, the report will note the n=1 condition and the report's per-relationship card will be suppressed under k-anonymity. The overall mean will still compute from the full rater list.

Self-assessment is a separate checkbox per subject. If self-assessment is on, ReliCheck auto-creates a self rater for that subject's email and the subject takes the survey on themselves. The self-vs-others gap diagnostic uses only the self rater, never imputed from peers.

Minute eight to eleven: launch invitations

Click Launch on the panel. One invitation is created per (subject, rater) pair. The system upserts every rater into the survey's contact list, sends a tokenized invitation that walks each rater through one survey response per subject they are evaluating, and tags each response with the relationship label (manager, peer, direct report, external, self) so the analytics can slice by rater group later. A manager evaluating three direct reports will see three invitations land in their inbox, one for each direct report being evaluated.

Two configuration points are worth a glance before the invitations go out. First, the reminder cadence. The default is a single reminder seven days after the first send, with a final reminder three days before the close date. For a 360 specifically, two reminders is the sweet spot. Three reminders starts to feel like nagging and depresses honest responses. Zero reminders leaves response rate where the calendar happened to land, which is usually under 60 percent.

Second, the close date. A 360 that stays open for more than three weeks loses momentum and the late responses tend to be hurried. Two weeks is the right default for a manager 360, three weeks for a senior-leader panel where calendars run dense.

Minute eleven to fourteen: while you wait

The first four steps are the active fifteen minutes. The next two weeks are the rater window. Three things are worth doing during the wait, none of which require returning to the survey configuration.

First, watch the completion-by-relationship pill on the panel page. ReliCheck tracks completion separately per rater type. If managers are at 90 percent and direct reports are at 30 percent two days before close, that is the bucket to nudge through a different channel. The default reminder hits everyone equally; manual nudges are warranted when a single bucket is lagging the others.

Second, brief the subjects. The strongest predictor of a useful 360 conversation is whether the subject knows what to expect before they read the report. A five-minute pre-brief that names the rater buckets, names the self-vs-others gap as a normal outcome rather than a problem, and frames the report as developmental rather than evaluative is the difference between a report that produces growth and a report that produces defensiveness.

Third, identify the coaching follow-through plan. The 360 report is most useful when paired with a follow-up coaching conversation inside two weeks of delivery. Knowing who is on the hook for that conversation, before the report drops, prevents the most common 360 failure mode: a thoughtful report that produces no action because no one owned the next step.

Minute fourteen to fifteen: the report

When the close date arrives (or when the response threshold you set is met), the subject report is available for every subject in the panel. Click any subject's name to open it. The report is one page, one subject, no comparisons across subjects.

Across the top is the AI summary paragraph. It names the strongest theme by quoting the highest-rated item, names the biggest development area by quoting the lowest, and flags meaningful gaps between the subject's self-rating and the rater-group means when self-assessment was included. The tone tier (Strong picture, Solid with notes, Gaps to address, Significant gaps) is the one-word read for whether the report is celebratory, mixed, or pointed.

Below the summary is the completion-by-relationship table, the eight-column item-means table (overall plus one column per relationship type plus a self-vs-others column), the top-three and bottom-three items by overall mean, and the open-ended comments grouped by question with the rater's relationship tagged. The Download report button at the top assembles the same content into a PDF the subject can keep.

One read of the report takes about five minutes per subject. Twelve subjects is an hour of reading. Most HR partners do that read before the coaching conversation, not during it, and arrive at the conversation with a working hypothesis about where the subject's development opportunity sits.

What the fifteen-minute claim is and is not

Fifteen minutes is the active configuration work: opening the suite, picking and customizing the starter, building the panel of subjects and raters, and launching invitations. It does not count the time raters spend completing the survey, the response-collection window, the subject's pre-brief, or the coaching follow-up. Those are the substantive steps a 360 has always required. The fifteen minutes is the part that used to be its own multi-week project: choosing the instrument, configuring the rater types, building separate forms per type, wiring the per-subject report logic, and trying to keep all of it in sync.

That coordination overhead is what the 360 Feedback Suite removes. The instrument is preselected, the rater types are first-class on the panel, the form runs once per (subject, rater) pair without per-type duplication, the report is computed from the same rater data without a consultant building it, and the analytics tab carries the self-vs-others diagnostic into a coaching conversation that can actually start the same day the report lands.

Start your panel inside the 360 Feedback Suite. The first one runs free on the starter plan; multi-panel cohorts move onto the team plan when the work warrants it.
