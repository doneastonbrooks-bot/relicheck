RReliCheck mockups
FACILITATOR SCRIPT

Watch a colleague walk the prototype.

Nine tasks, about 25 minutes total. Read each prompt aloud, then stay quiet. The goal is to find what trips people up while the fixes are still cheap.

Open the launcher Print this script

Before you start

  • Open index.html on the colleague's screen. They drive. You watch.
  • Tell them: "This is a clickable design. Some things won't work. Just say out loud what you'd expect to happen, and I'll tell you if it's broken or just not wired up yet."
  • Promise not to coach. If they ask "what do I click?", say "what would you try?"
  • Keep this script open on a second screen and jot answers as you go.
  • If they hesitate for more than about 10 seconds, that's data. Note it. Then ask "what are you looking for right now?"
Part 1 . Orientation (5 minutes)
1
PROMPT

"Take 30 seconds to look at this page. What is ReliCheck for?"

If they ask for more: "Just first impressions. No wrong answers."
Watch for: do they read the launcher's framing, or skip to the tiles? Do they say "surveys," "stats," "analytics," "AI"? The answer tells you whether the positioning lands or needs sharper wording.
Stays on index.html
2
PROMPT

"Where would you click to start?"

If they hover on multiple tiles: stay silent. The hesitation is the finding.
Watch for: do they pick Home, Create / Upload, or Projects? People who click Projects expect a workspace; people who click Create are starting from scratch. Both are valid. Note which.
Likely home.html or create-upload.html
Part 2 . Building a survey (7 minutes)
3
PROMPT

"You want to start a new employee engagement survey. Show me how."

Watch for: do they go through Home's quick action, the sidebar, or the breadcrumb? On Create / Upload, do they reach for the left column (build inside) or the right (upload)? "Use a template" should be a strong pull for the new-survey scenario.
Path: home → create-upload → builder
4
PROMPT

"On the builder page, can you tell what each part of the screen is for?"

Point at the purple Purpose card: "What does this do?"
Watch for: does the purple Purpose card read as required or optional? Do they notice items are grouped by construct, or does it look like flat questions? Does the AI Assist rail look like a tool palette or like ads?
builder.html
Part 3 . Reading results (8 minutes)
5
PROMPT

"You just got 412 responses back on this survey. Tell me whether the data is good enough to use."

Then ask: "How did you decide?"
Watch for: do they read the SSI ring (78/100), the verdict pill ("USE WITH MINOR CAUTIONS"), or skip both and look for the table? The point of the Guided view is to answer this question in five seconds. If it takes longer, the hierarchy is off.
analysis-guided.html
6
PROMPT

"Find the one thing you'd want to fix before the next survey."

Watch for: do they read the 5-step layered finding card top to bottom, or jump around? Do they open the "Show reliability statistics" disclosure, or trust the plain-language version? Do they understand what "Items 4 and 9" refers to?
Same page. Layered finding card under the SSI.
7
PROMPT

"Pretend you're a stats person. Show me where the actual numbers live."

Watch for: do they find the Guided / Technical toggle in the top right? Many testers miss it on the first pass. If they do, do they recognize the Technical view as the same data in a denser frame?
analysis-technical.html
Part 4 . Sharing (4 minutes)
8
PROMPT

"Your boss wants a one-page summary on Friday. How would you give them one?"

Watch for: do they hit the "Share" button in the top right, the "Add to report" in the layered card, or navigate over to the Reports sidebar? All three should work. If none feel right, the path needs a sharper signpost.
reports.html → report-detail.html
9
PROMPT

"A respondent asks 'will my answers be private?' Where would you look?"

Watch for: do they go to Account, Team, Help, or the Take view? The respondent-facing trust footer on take-survey.html is the most honest answer. Note whether they think to check the page a respondent actually sees.

5-minute debrief

  1. "What would you have called this product, in your own words?" Tells you whether the positioning landed.
  2. "Was anything frustrating?" Open-ended. People rarely volunteer this without being asked.
  3. "Would you have used this at your last job?" Probes fit, not features.
  4. "What was missing?" Catches the gap between the prototype and a real workflow.
  5. "On a scale of 1-10, how likely would you be to recommend ReliCheck to a colleague who needs to run a survey, and why that number?" The "why" matters more than the number.
