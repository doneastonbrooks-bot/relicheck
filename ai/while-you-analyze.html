Understand what the results actually mean.

Every dashboard opens with a plain-language read of the numbers underneath. A non-statistician can act on the page; a researcher can audit the read because every claim is tied to a specific number, item, or test that is one click away. Twelve assists cover the everyday tabs, the advanced methodologies accreditation reviewers expect, and the specialty surfaces (360 reviews, trends, equity gaps) that need their own translation.

The everyday reads

Six assists that sit on the dashboards you'll open most. Each one tells you what the page means before you read the table.

🛡

Reliability Brief

See whether your survey items are consistently measuring the same thing.

The opening paragraph on the Reliability tab names the scale's internal consistency in plain language, points to the weakest items by their prompt text, and recommends a next step. Under the surface: Cronbach's alpha, McDonald's omega, item-total correlation, and split-half reliability.

Top of the Reliability analytics tab.

¶

Tab Briefs

Open any analytics tab and you'll already know what the page is saying.

A plain-language paragraph sits at the top of Description, Validity, Open-Ended, Compare, Pre/Post, Subgroups, and Predictors. Description covers response patterns and ceiling effects; Validity translates the factor structure; Open-Ended describes engagement without touching respondent text; Compare interprets group differences; Pre/Post explains learning gains; Subgroups names the biggest gaps; Predictors reports what drives the outcome.

Top of the seven everyday tabs.

Key Drivers Brief

Find out what is actually moving the outcome.

The Brief names the top one to three drivers by their actual labels and reports each one in plain numbers ("workload accounts for 28 percent of the explained variance in engagement"). A correlated-but-redundant predictor is flagged as a multicollinearity story rather than a finding. Under the surface: Johnson's Relative Weights for continuous outcomes, standardized log-odds for binary outcomes.

Top of the Key Drivers analytics tab.

⚒

Response Quality Brief

See whether the responses are trustworthy enough to act on.

The paragraph at the top of the Response Quality dashboard reads in plain HR voice ("data quality is solid; two respondents straight-lined every Likert item and one open-ended question is being skipped at 38 percent"). Channel-level skew is called out when one distribution path is attracting noticeably lower-effort responses. Individual respondents are never identified. Under the surface: straight-lining counts, duplicate-vector pairs, low-effort open-end share, per-question missingness.

Top of the Response Quality analytics tab.

↷

Completion Brief

Find out where people are dropping off and why.

The opening paragraph names the Completion Score, points to the drop-off question by its actual prompt text, and calls out any item skipped at a much higher rate than the rest. When a grouping variable is present, a group with a sharply higher skip rate gets flagged so accessibility or engagement gaps surface before they become a story.

Top of the Completion analytics tab.

Trends Brief

Know whether a change between waves is real or noise.

On the Trends dashboard, the opening paragraph reports whether the composite is up, steady, or slipping, names the most-moved construct by its actual label, and translates the significance flag into plain English ("the drop is statistically significant" / "the change is within sampling noise" / "the sample is too small to call this significant yet"). When wave detection comes from the Pulse channel-tag convention, the wave labels are referenced directly, so the read aligns with how the survey was sent.

Top of the Trends analytics tab (in More analyses).

✔

Readiness Brief

Find out whether your survey is ready to send before the first response is in.

The Survey Readiness tab grades the survey design across six weighted domains and the paragraph translates the score into a plain answer: ready to send, almost ready with two small fixes, workable with real gaps, or not ready until blockers clear. The single most pressing fix is named by its issue title or domain, with a reference to what unlocks if it gets fixed (omega, Compare, per-construct reliability, equity gaps). At 85 or higher, the fix-first framing is dropped entirely.

Top of the Readiness analytics tab, the Distribute pre-publish card, and the survey ctx-bar.

How every Brief is shaped

Same structure on every dashboard, so the read stays predictable.

The headline tells you what the page means. The body paragraph names the actual variables. The closing line points to a next step. If a result is thin or the sample is small, that gets said before the headline; numbers come from the live analytics snapshot, never invented.

Shared structure across all twelve assists in this stage.

More on the verdict tiers

Every Verdict Card uses the same four tiers, so a reader who learns them once can read any dashboard. Reliability reads as Strong, Adequate, Modest, or Weak. Measurement invariance reads as Yes, Compare with care, Not on equal footing, or Not yet ready. The body paragraph always names the actual outcome and grouping variable, so the read is about your data, not a template. Predictors reads as "A 1-point increase in Manager support is associated with about a 0.4-point increase in Engagement (standardized beta = 0.41)." Mediation, moderation, group comparison, pre/post change, subgroup gaps, key drivers, IRT, and MLM all use the same shape.

More on the methodology coverage

Six methodologies, each with its own paragraph at the top of its analytics card.

  • Confirmatory factor analysis reads as whether the survey structure holds up. Under the surface: CFI, TLI, RMSEA, SRMR.
  • Measurement invariance answers whether the survey works the same way across groups.
  • Item response theory translates discrimination and item information into "this item separates higher and lower scorers" language. Under the surface: Graded Response, 2PL, 3PL, and two-dimensional MIRT, including which items cross-load on two traits.
  • Mediation reports the indirect effect with bootstrap confidence. Under the surface: BCa intervals, log-odds scale for binary outcomes.
  • Moderation reports where the effect is real and where it isn't. Under the surface: the Johnson-Neyman region.
  • Multilevel models answer whether the grouping (classrooms, teams, sites) matters. Under the surface: ICC and variance components for two-level linear, two-level logistic GLMM, and three-level linear models.

More on the suite roll-up

The quarterly Brief is built to be the line you would actually read aloud in a leadership meeting. The paragraph names specific constructs and surveys ("HR Suite is steady this quarter; engagement is essentially flat, exit volume is up modestly, response volume slipped 12 percent"), and a tone pill (Steady or improving, Mixed quarter, Watch this quarter, Slipping) gives the one-word read. Under the surface: quarter-over-quarter response volume, average Strength Index across the suite, and Likert-mean direction on any construct tagged across two or more surveys. Numbers come from the live response data, never invented.

More on the 360 Subject Brief

For every subject of a multi-rater panel, a two-to-four sentence summary sits at the top of the subject report. The strongest theme gets named by quoting the highest-rated item; the biggest development area by quoting the lowest. Blind spots are flagged when self-ratings exceed others by 0.75 points or more on the five-point scale (or in the reverse direction). The closing line nudges what the manager or HR partner should do next, and a tone pill (Strong picture, Solid with notes, Gaps to address, Significant gaps) gives a one-glance read before any number is shown.

The full subject report is also downloadable as a PDF for in-person review with the subject or a manager.

Back to the card

More on the Equity Gaps Brief

The Equity Gap analysis tab reports outcome differences across protected-class or program groups (gender, race or ethnicity, age band, role, tenure, etc.). Axes appear by the survey's actual question label; groups appear by their actual option labels; effect sizes get translated into plain English ("engagement is 0.71 standard deviations lower for one group than another on the race or ethnicity axis"). The framing is patterns to understand and act on, never accusations.

When subgroups were hidden for k-anonymity, the hidden count is noted once so the reader knows a smaller-group story may be missing. Under the surface: Cohen's d, k-anonymity thresholds.
