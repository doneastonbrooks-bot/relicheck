ReliCheck Survey Strength Index™

One number that answers: can this survey be trusted?

The ReliCheck Survey Strength Index (SSI™) is the 0 to 100 composite score that rolls up reliability, factor structure, item quality, response quality, open-ended depth, and actionability into a single defensible verdict. The math is transparent, the bands are public, and every survey gets the same yardstick.

A single, comparable score across every survey you run.

A reliability coefficient, a KMO, a Bartlett's, a missing-data rate, a count of duplicates: each tells part of the story. The SSI™ combines them with weights derived from peer-reviewed measurement practice, so a reader without a stats background can still read the verdict and trust the math underneath it.

The six domains

How the 100 points are allocated.

Each domain awards points based on conventional psychometric thresholds. The weights reflect how much each domain affects whether a result holds up under scrutiny.

Reliability

25

Anchored on McDonald's omega when it can be estimated, with Cronbach's alpha as the fallback. Per-construct reliability and the alpha to omega gap shape the final allocation.

Inputs: omega · alpha · SEM · per-construct alpha

Factor structure

20

Whether the items group into factors that match the constructs they were written to measure. Pulls from KMO, Bartlett's significance, variance explained, and confirmatory fit when CFA has been run.

Inputs: KMO · Bartlett's · variance explained · CFA fit

Item quality

20

Per-item diagnostics. Rewards strong item-total correlations and clear discrimination; penalizes ceiling and floor effects, low-variance items, and items that hurt the scale on alpha-if-deleted.

Inputs: item-total r · ceiling/floor · alpha-if-deleted

Response quality

15

Whether the responses themselves look honest. Counts straight-lining, duplicate response vectors, missing-data rates, and sample size adequacy for the analyses being run.

Inputs: straight-lining · duplicates · missingness · n

Open-ended depth

10

For surveys with open-ended items, rewards engagement and average response length. Surveys with no open-ended items skip the domain and the weight is redistributed proportionally.

Inputs: response rate · average length · theme coverage

Actionability

10

Whether the survey produces decisions a stakeholder can act on. Rewards clear construct labeling, paired groups for comparison, and outcome variables for predictor or driver analysis.

Inputs: construct tags · group variables · outcome columns

Why this design

Why a composite score, instead of a list of statistics?

A reliability coefficient lives next to a KMO, which lives next to an item-total correlation, which lives next to a missing-data rate. Any one of them can be strong while another quietly fails. A reader who only checks alpha can publish a survey whose factor structure does not hold up; a reader who only checks KMO can publish one with a third of respondents straight-lining the items.

The SSI™ is the answer to a practical question that researchers ask anyway: given everything we know about this survey, can it be trusted? Rolling the diagnostics into a single composite forces the analyst to face the weakest link instead of cherry-picking the strongest. Weighting the domains by their conventional importance gives the score a defensible basis without claiming false precision.

The score is not a replacement for reading the report. The Reliability tab still shows alpha, omega, item-total correlations, and SEM. The Validity tab still shows EFA, CFA, and invariance. The SSI™ sits on top of those, summarizing the verdict so a stakeholder who does not read the technical appendix still gets the right answer.

Where the weights came from

The 25/20/20/15/10/10 allocation reflects three principles. First, reliability is the prerequisite for everything else, so it gets the largest single share. Second, factor structure and item quality together (40 points) reflect that "the right items, grouped the right way" is the second-most-important question after "the items are stable." Third, response quality and actionability are independent of the items themselves, so they round out the score without dominating it.

What the score does not measure

The SSI™ does not score the meaning of the result, the ethics of the sampling frame, the appropriateness of the construct, or the quality of the decision a stakeholder makes from the data. It scores measurement integrity. A survey with a 95 SSI™ can still be the wrong instrument for the question being asked. The numbers help; they do not replace the analyst.

Common questions

Reading the score in practice.

What if my survey has no Likert items?

The Reliability and Factor Structure domains require Likert items to compute. If the survey is entirely open-ended, single-choice, or behavioral, those domains are skipped and the remaining domains are reweighted proportionally. The SSI™ still produces a score, anchored on response quality, open-ended depth, and actionability.

How many responses do I need before the score is reliable?

The score itself can be computed at any sample size. For the verdict tier to be defensible, aim for at least 30 responses per construct for internal consistency, and at least 100 responses overall for the Response Quality domain to clear its full-credit threshold. The dashboard flags small samples explicitly so the score is read in context.

Why did my SSI™ drop after I added more responses?

Two common reasons. First, the new responses may have introduced more straight-lining or missing data, dragging Response Quality down. Second, additional respondents can shift the factor structure, especially when the new respondents come from a different population than the original sample. The Strength Index tab's narrator names the specific domain that moved.

Can two surveys with the same SSI™ have very different profiles?

Yes. A survey at 78 with strong reliability and weaker open-ended depth has a different remediation path than a survey at 78 with strong response quality and weaker factor structure. The single number is the headline; the domain breakdown is where the actual diagnosis lives.

Is the SSI™ comparable across different surveys?

Within ReliCheck, yes. The same yardstick applies to every survey, so a 75 on a customer feedback instrument means the same level of measurement integrity as a 75 on an employee engagement survey. Across instruments measuring different constructs, the score is a measurement-integrity comparison, not a content comparison.

How does the SSI™ relate to the "Can I Use These Results?" advisor?

The advisor sits underneath the SSI™ ring on the Strength Index tab. It bundles the score, response quality flags, and sample size into a four-tier recommendation: yes, yes with cautions, use with care, or not yet. The SSI™ is the underlying measurement; the advisor is the practical guidance.

Can I see the formula?

Yes. The full per-domain scoring rules are documented in the methodology page, including the partial-credit interpolation and the redistribution rules for surveys without open-ended items. The intent is that any researcher can audit the math.
